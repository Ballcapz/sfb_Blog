21 July 2018

Part 5 - Carving the Shape

This was one of the longest and most strenuous parts of the creation of this board. It has taken me around a week in order to get to a point where I am somewhat happy with the shape of the board. It's not perfect, but I know it never will be and it's good enough for who it's for.

Part 5 - I started out making some progress with the bottom rocker, or the curve of the bottom of the board. I am only using my Surform along with starting out from a square blank. With both factors in play, it took me quite a while to come up with a shape resembling a board.


After that, I started in on the top, and this ended up taking twice as long as the bottom because the center of the stringer, the thickest part, was even with the bottom of the board. This gave me twice as much foam on top compared to the bottom.





Here is also a side profile of the board where you can see the rocker shape a little bit better. As shown, I used a fairly flat rocker, reason being it fit better in a square 4 inch piece of foam and it is a bit of a crossover with a stand-up paddle board.


After several days of work, I finally reached the point that all the pros were saying is the hardest part of shaping, the rails. Boy were they right! The work on the rails did not take me as much time as the other parts, but they were definitley the most difficult by far. I started out with marking some 'Rail Bands' with a pencil with some guides that I found online. Then I started sanding down until I had something resembling a rail of a surfboard. Then it was just a few short hours of finetuning and sanding until I got to a point where I was happy with the shape. It is definitely not perfect by any means, but through all the research that I have done on the topic one of the number one pieces of advice that professionals have given is to not keep working and working until all you have left is a pile of sanded foam. I know that I will keep improving in the future, but for right now i'll have a board that floats.


After that, I used some very fine sandpaper to smooth out a bunch of little imperfections, and now I am finally ready to get to the glassing of my board!

12 July 2018

Part 4 - Outline & Beginning Shape

I was finally able to get started on cutting out the shape of my board and starting to shape it after waiting for the glue to dry for 24 hours. Having that time did allow me to make some more plans for what I was doing with the board though.

Part 4 - Today my surfboard is finally starting to look a little bit more like a surfboard! The first thing that I did tonight was to cut out the template that I used for the shape of the board.


I was able to download the free template from Blending Curves, which is an awesome website with tons of resources for shaping boards. Next, I laid out the template on my square blank and traced it out with a sharpie.


After taking measurements and making sure both sides were equal distances from the stringer and centered on the stringr, I began to cut it.




After I had it rough cut, I used my Surform and Sandpaper to smooth out and finsh shaping the edges of the board so both sides were symemetrical and smooth.


Finally, I was able to get started on planing down the foam to create the shape of my board. For my first board, I am just using hand tools to do the shaping, so I started in with my Surform and made some good progress in a short amount of time.


I am excited to see how far I can get on the shaping by the end of this weekend, hopefully within a week or two I will have a waterproof and floating board!

11 July 2018

Part 2 & 3 - Glue

The last few days have been busier than usual for me, so I am just now getting to an update on my board. This evening I reached the point of gluing a stringiner in the middle of the board. Hopefully tomorrow I can begin to shape it!

Part 2 - The first thing that I did yesterday was to set up my shaping bay in my barn. It took quite a bit of time, as I built two shaping stands, as well as sectioning off an area with clear poly, but I am pretty happy with the current set up that I have.




After that I measured to the center of the board, and clamped on a guide board to keep my saw straight while I was cutting the board. To cut the board, I used a Dewalt Circular Saw with the blade on backwards to cut the foam. With the blade on backwards, it doesn't chew up the foam as bad giving a much smoother cut. After that, I printed out a template for a stringer online, and cut out my stringer out of 3/16" plywood.


Finally, I placed the stringer onto one half where I wanted it and glued it down with some Gorilla Glue. I had to switch glues because the gorilla glue foams up a little bit and helps to make a nice, tight seal between the two pieces that are being glued.


Part 3 - Today's work was pretty quick and light compared to the last few days of working on the board. Today I glued the other half of the board onto the half with the stringer already on it, and set it up to dry overnight. First I sanded down some excess glue that was on the first half, then I glued and placed the other half on top of the first one. I put a 2x4 on the top edge, and used ratchet straps to hold and clamp the two halves together.


If everything turns out as planned, I will be starting on the shaping of the board tomorrow!

8 July 2018

Part 1 - XPS Foam

Today was the first day of my new summer project and possible hobby. I started the process of building and shaping surf boards and Stand-up paddle boards.

I started research for this board about a week ago when I saw the cost of some Stand-up paddle boards online. Then I got a look at the setup of one of my extended relatives and decided that this was a hobby that I wanted to pick up. So off to Youtube I went. I surfed the web to find many different ideas and techniques I could use for my first board. What I came up with is that I might as well start from scratch, or as close to it as possible. I decided to start with making the foam blank.

I bought a 2 inch thick piece of Extruded Polystyrene (XPS) foam from Home Depot this afternoon and got started on setting up my area and the first steps of making the blank as soon as I returned home. First I measured and marked and squared up the center of the sheet of XPS.


Then I sawed it in half with a hand saw.


After that I squared the two pieces up and smoothed out the rough edges with a Surform and sandpaper. Next, I sanded the two faces of the foam that were going to be glued together, and covered the face of one of the boards with Liquid Nails construction adhesive.






I used a ratchet strap to hold together the center of the board, then I placed it on the ground and placed some weights on top of it to keep good pressure on the board while the glue dries.


Tomorrow the strap and weights will be removed and we'll see how it turns out!

8 July 2018

My Tools and Supplies

I figured I'd throw in another post tonight. Here are some of the tools and supplies that I am using as of today.

This first tool that I use to shape and to smooth out the board is a standard Surform. The model that I use is a Stanley 21-296 Surform Plane

Stanley 21-296 Surform Plane

The adhesive that I have been using to stick everything together is Liquid Nails Construction Adhesive. I get mine in the 10oz. varity, and use a caulking gun to apply it nice and spread out. This stuff has worked wonders so far and is fairly inexpensive for what you get.
Liquid Nails

Next we have the foam that I use for the main body of the board. This is just regular construction polystyrene foam. The brand I got is Owens Corning Fomular 250 XPS foam. The reason for the 250 over the 150 is the 10 psi higher density of the 250.

Another one of the main tools that I use is a standard Stanley 15in Fat Max Hand Saw. It cuts like a dream and I use it to cut the foam in half as well as cut the rough shape of the board.
Stanley Fat Max Hand Saw

Last but not least, we can not forget our sandpaper. At the beginning, I have been using a 60 or 40 grit to rough up the foam a little bit and get some of the edges close to equal to eachother. Some of the real smoothing will come later on in the process. My favorite sandpaper so far has been 3M Pro Grade.

3M 60 Grit Pro Grade No-Slip Sandpaper
